Two new courses start this month

10/4/2015

 
This month two new course related to Robotics starts.

First one is Robotics Vision, the second MOOC of Peter Corke. He is an Australian roboticist known for his work on Visual Servoing, field robotics and the MATLAB Toolboxes for Robotics and Machine Vision. The course is focus on Artificial Vision for Robotics and it is based on some chapters of his book 'Robotics, Vision & Control'.

Second course is How stuff moves (III): Wave calculus, in it how oscillating systems move is explained. This is the third part of the 'How stuff moves' series, part 1 examined linear motion while part 2 examined angular motion. Although the third part is not directly related to Robotics, it is good to complete the full series to understand the mechanics of all types of physical systems.

You could see when the courses start in our calendar.

New community in G+ 

29/3/2015

 
Although some time has passed since our last post, it does not mean that we have not been working. During this period we have worked, in addition to our continuous review courses and resources, in preparing the infrastructure of the school.

We are still working on it but you could see our two main improvements:
  • Now the school have it's own address: open-robotics-school.org
  • And we have created a community in G+ to talk/support you.

In this community you could interact with other people interested in learning Robotics. You could ask questions about courses and resources you are doing, and doubts you have; and other students or the ORS staff will support you.

Moreover, you could help others publishing resources to learn Robotics, including new courses. By adding these resources you are also helping the ORS to find new material.

Two new courses announced by edX

28/3/2014

 
The following courses have been announced by the edX.
  • Robot Mechanics and Control, Part I by SNU.
  • Autonomous Navigation for Flying Robots by the TUM.

The Robot Mechanics and Control course provides a mathematical introduction to the mechanics and control of robots that can be modeled as kinematic chains. Topics covered include the concept of a robot’s configuration space and degrees of freedom, static grasp analysis, the description of rigid body motions, kinematics of open and closed chains, and the basics of robot control.

While in the Autonomous Navigation for Flying Robots course, the basic concepts for autonomous navigation with quadrotors (including topics such as probabilistic state estimation, linear control, and path planning) will be introduced.
